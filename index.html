key idea
Furstenberg's Argument, Generalised to Monoids

In Furstenberg's 1955 proof of the infinitude of primes, the core observation is that $\mathbb{Z}\setminus\{\pm1\} = \bigcup_{p\,\in\,\mathrm{Irr}^{\times}(\mathbb{Z})} p\mathbb{Z}$ — every nonunit integer is divisible by some prime. Notice that $\mathbb{Z}$ here is only used as a multiplicative monoid: the cosets $p\mathbb{Z}$ are monoid cosets, and the topology is defined by arithmetic progressions. The additive group structure plays no role.

We apply exactly this pattern to a different monoid. The challenge is: find a monoid whose irreducibles correspond to twin prime averages — i.e., to integers $x$ where $6x+1$ and $6x-1$ are both $\pm$prime, so that $(6x-1,\,6x+1)$ is a twin prime pair.

The answer is the anti-diagonal submonoid $N = \Delta(\mathbb{Z}) \subset (\mathbb{Z}^2,\otimes)$, where $\otimes$ is built from the operations $\circledast$ and $\star$. The isomorphisms $\phi(x)=6x+1$ and $\psi(x)=6x-1$ transfer irreducibility in $N$ to primality of both $6x\pm1$, establishing the bijection $\mathrm{Irr}(N) \leftrightarrow \{\text{twin prime pairs}\}$.

With this monoid in hand, the Furstenberg argument applies verbatim: cover $N\setminus\{1_N\}$ by irreducible cosets, equip $N$ with a coset topology where each coset is closed and every nonempty open set is infinite, and conclude $|\mathrm{Irr}(N)|=\infty$ by contradiction.

$$\underbrace{\mathbb{Z}\setminus\{\pm1\}=\bigcup_{p\in\mathbb{P}} p\mathbb{Z}}_{\text{Furstenberg (primes)}} \qquad\longrightarrow\qquad \underbrace{N\setminus\{1_N\}=\bigcup_{q\in\mathrm{Irr}(N)} q\otimes N}_{\text{This paper (twin primes)}}$$
